Comparison

## Overview (John 16:16–33) Across Campuses

All three campuses preach from the same Upper Room Discourse text and center on Jesus preparing disciples (and believers today) for **tribulation**, **sorrow turning to joy**, and **peace/courage in Christ** ("take heart").

## Shared Theological Throughline

- **Jesus prepares disciples for absence → resurrection joy**: Each campus highlights "a little while" as the near-term arc of cross → resurrection, with sorrow giving way to joy.
- **Tribulation is normal for Christians**: All three treat "in the world you will have tribulation" as a promise/expectation rather than an exception.
- **Peace and courage are found "in me" (in Christ)**: All land the passage on Jesus’ stated purpose: "that in me you may have peace" and "I have overcome the world."
- **Prayer in Jesus’ name as post-ascension provision**: Each emphasizes a new/pronounced pattern of prayer ("ask…in my name") connected to joy.

## Application Emphases (Where They Lean)

### Boulder (Perry Marshall)

- **Confidence to endure trouble comes from God’s faithfulness, not ours**: Strong emphasis that "our confidence…does not depend on our performance" and "The faithfulness of God is greater than our failures." Application targets shame, discouragement, and fear of not measuring up.
- **God cares about emotions, not just facts**: Repeated pastoral attention to Jesus knowing "how you're feeling" (sadness/anxiety/joy) and meeting people there.
- **Prayer shaped by the Father’s love**: Emphasis on God’s posture toward the believer—"The Father himself loves you"—with direct reassurance (not annoyed/bothered).
- **Community framing**: Ends by noting Jesus speaks to the disciples "as a community together," and frames endurance as collective.

### Erie (Tom Shirk)

- **Christological clarity + mission statement focus**: The center of gravity is verse 28 as a "Christological mission statement" ("I came from the Father… leaving… going to the Father"). The application is: if you grasp who Jesus is, what he accomplished, and where he is now, you can stand in tribulation.
- **More direct evangelistic call**: Explicitly presses being "in Christ" ("If you are holding Christ at arm's length, you're not in him"), tying peace to conversion (forgiveness, adoption, Spirit indwelling).
- **Prayer with realism about unanswered prayer**: He addresses discouragement and frames answers as "yes…no…wait," warning against praying "in our name."
- **Uncertainty as a dominant felt-need**: Uses contemporary uncertainty (geopolitical conflict, markets, AI) to connect disciples’ confusion to modern anxiety.

### Thornton (Brody Young)

- **Joy theology: joy vs. happiness**: Distinct emphasis that joy is not circumstance-based but "based on a person" (Jesus). Develops a structured set of claims: joy "outweighs" sorrow, "outlasts" sorrow, and "overflows" ("full").
- **Eternity-shaped perspective**: Frames joy as viewing the present "against all of eternity"; sorrow becomes "a distant memory" when seen in resurrection/kingdom/new creation.
- **Prayer as the pathway to joy**: Strong rhetorical repetition that Jesus keeps "pounding that drum"—"pray, pray, pray"—and explicitly connects prayer access to "joy…full." Uses an access/"address" illustration to make prayer concrete.
- **Mission/kingdom illustration**: Uses St. Patrick to show prayer-shaped endurance producing spiritual impact beyond circumstances.

## Illustrations & Stories (Notable Differences)

- **Boulder**: Memorial service for a role model ("Roger") to frame "last words" and preparation; plus a short humorous aside about Jesus using childbirth as an analogy ("boldest teaching"). Also uses a Gerald Ford / authority-in-a-name story to illustrate praying "in Jesus’ name."
- **Erie**: Extended uncertainty examples (Iran/markets/AI/March Madness bracket) to empathize with anxiety; long personal childbirth/labor story (stuck in third gear; church van) to color the childbirth analogy; airplane turbulence as a picture of trusting the one in control.
- **Thornton**: Opens with a research study about perceived time in depression vs happiness; revisits John 2 temple-cleansing placement to show Jesus has long prepared disciples for death/resurrection; middle-school basketball letter "no address" story to illustrate access to the Father; St. Patrick narrative as a lived example of prayer-formed joy and mission.

## Tone & Delivery (As Reflected in Transcript)

- **Boulder**: Pastoral, tender, counseling-oriented; frequent reassurance and emotional attunement. Emphasizes comfort and stability (peace, Father’s love).
- **Erie**: Teacher-preacher with doctrinal framing and cultural commentary; candid, occasionally humorous, and exhortational. Strong imperatives: don’t "fall away," be "in Christ," "don’t quit praying."
- **Thornton**: Energetic, youth/young-adult accessible, illustration-rich; emphasizes inner life transformation (joy) and invites people into prayerful dependence.

## Call-to-Action / Closing Moves

- **Boulder**: Calls people to trust God in trouble through remembering Jesus’ victory, relying on Father’s love and Spirit’s guidance; frames endurance as communal; closes with prayer for discouraged people.
- **Erie**: Clear call to be "in Christ" (conversion/assurance) and then to persevere: "Don't give up… Don't fall away… Don't quit praying… You're loved by God."
- **Thornton**: Main imperative: "Take heart. Be courageous." Practical next step is turning to Jesus as the "source of our joy" through prayer; closes with dependence language ("we're weak… would you be strong?").

## Theology / Interpretation Differences

- Erie more explicitly frames verse 28 as a formal "Christological mission statement" and ties application to having correct Christology ("God of very God… eternally existing"), whereas Boulder/Thornton treat verse 28 as clarifying context but don’t foreground metaphysical language to the same degree.
- Erie makes a more explicit conversion boundary around being "in Christ" ("If you haven't yielded your life to him… you're not in him"), while Boulder and Thornton apply the promises primarily to the existing believer community without the same explicit gatekeeping language.

## Material Differences in What Was Said

- Boulder uniquely emphasizes **God’s faithfulness over our failures** ("does not depend on our performance") and highlights Jesus’ emotional care (Jesus knows "how you're feeling").
- Erie uniquely includes extensive contemporary uncertainty examples (Iran, markets, AI) and an airplane turbulence analogy, plus a long personal labor/delivery story.
- Thornton uniquely develops a **joy vs happiness** framework and a three-part structure (joy "outweighs/outlasts/overflows"), includes the John 2 temple narrative connection, and uses St. Patrick as a major illustration of prayer-shaped endurance and mission.
